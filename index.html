Scientific Definition

What is Dermatophytosis?

Dermatophytosis, commonly known as Ringworm, is a superficial fungal infection affecting keratinized tissues including hair, skin, and claws. The fungi responsible are keratinophilic, meaning they utilize keratin as their primary nutrient. This adaptation allows them to persist on surfaces that are typically resistant to other microorganisms.

Impact on Animals & Humans

  • Manifestations differ by species, age, immune status, and environment.
  • Zoonotic potential: Can spread to humans, especially in multi-animal households or clinics.
  • Young and immunocompromised animals are more susceptible.

Clinical Awareness

The infection often begins with subtle signs, making awareness and detailed clinical observation essential. Dermatophytosis affects animal health and also poses a public health concern in households, clinics, and livestock farms.

Mechanism of Infection

Infection Process

  • Adherence: Arthrospores attach to corneocytes via specific adhesion molecules.
  • Germination: Spores germinate forming hyphae that penetrate the stratum corneum.
  • Enzymatic Digestion: Proteases and keratinases degrade keratin → nutrients for fungi.
  • Immune Response: Inflammation → erythema, scaling, pustules. Chronic → hyperkeratosis.

Environmental Influence

Humidity, temperature, and animal density significantly affect disease severity.

Bio-Mechanics & Transmission

The Bio-Mechanics of Infection (The Arthrospore)

  • The Infective Unit: Infection is driven by Arthrospores, which are formed by the fragmentation of fungal hyphae within the hair and skin.
  • Environmental Resilience: Arthrospores possess a thick cell wall that protects them against desiccation and UV light. They remain viable and infectious for up to 18–24 months in the environment.
  • The "Clinging" Mechanism: Spores are coated with a proteinaceous sticky substance, allowing them to adhere firmly to keratinized surfaces (furniture, brushes, and fabrics).
  • The Silent Threat (Asymptomatic Carriers): Subclinically infected animals (especially long-haired cats) can harbor thousands of spores in their fur without showing lesions, acting as a primary reservoir for outbreaks.

Routes of Transmission (The Dynamics)

  • Direct Transmission (Skin-to-Skin): Occurs through physical contact between an infected host and a susceptible one. High risk during nursing, grooming, or mating.
  • Indirect Transmission (The Role of Fomites):
    • House Dust: Infected hairs and scales become part of the dust, making the entire environment a source of infection.
    • Contaminated Tools: Grooming shears, clippers, and brushes are the #1 vectors in clinics and shelters.
    • Micro-abrasions: Clippers often cause tiny skin nicks that act as "gateways" for immediate fungal entry.
  • Environmental (Geophilic) Route: Exposure to contaminated soil containing species like M. gypseum. Infection typically occurs on the muzzle and paws during digging.

Causative Pathogens

Microsporum canis

Primary dermatophyte in cats, responsible for zoonotic infections. Highly contagious among kittens and humans.

Trichophyton verrucosum

Prevalent in cattle; produces thick, crusty lesions. Infection can persist in herds if untreated.

Microsporum gypseum

Geophilic fungus from soil. Causes sporadic infections in dogs and cats exposed to contaminated environments.

Advanced Clinical Manifestations

Feline

Circular alopecia with fine scaling ("cigarette ash" appearance). Some cats are asymptomatic carriers, spreading spores.

Canine

Classic ring lesions with crusting. Often accompanied by secondary bacterial infections, leading to pruritus and discomfort.

Bovine

Thick greyish-white crusts mainly on the head, neck, and limbs. Can affect productivity and weight gain in livestock.

Diagnostic Protocols

Wood's Lamp

Fluorescence seen in 50-60% of Microsporum canis strains; helps in rapid initial assessment.

Trichogram

Examination of hair shafts in 10-20% KOH allows identification of fungal elements.

Dermatophyte Test Medium (DTM)

Color change from orange to red indicates alkaline metabolites from dermatophytes, confirming infection.

Laboratory Diagnostics

Direct Microscopic Examination (KOH Prep):

  • Skin scrapings, hair, or nail clippings are placed in a 10% to 20% Potassium Hydroxide (KOH) solution.
  • The Science: The KOH dissolves keratin but leaves fungal hyphae and spores intact.
  • Observation: Look for branching, septate hyphae under microscope.

Physiological & Advanced Tests:

  • In-vitro Hair Perforation Test: Differentiates T. mentagrophytes (Positive) from T. rubrum (Negative).
  • Positive result shows wedge-shaped perforations stained with LPCB.
  • PCR (Molecular Detection): Detects fungal DNA directly from samples, fast result (1-3 days) with high sensitivity.

Treatment & Control

The choice of treatment depends primarily on the location and severity of the infection.

A. Topical Treatments (For Skin and Body)

Used for localized infections like Tinea Corporis (body) or Tinea Pedis (athlete's foot).

  • Antifungal Creams/Ointments: Over-the-counter or prescription options containing Terbinafine, Clotrimazole, or Ketoconazole.
  • Application: Apply the cream to the lesion and about 2 cm of healthy skin surrounding it. Continue use for at least 7–14 days after the skin appears clear to prevent recurrence.

B. Oral Medications (For Scalp and Nails)

Topical creams cannot penetrate hair shafts or nail beds effectively.

  • Tinea Capitis (Scalp): Requires oral antifungal medications (e.g., Griseofulvin or Terbinafine) for 4–8 weeks.
  • Onychomycosis (Nails): May require oral treatment for 3–6 months.
  • Note: Oral antifungals may require periodic blood tests to monitor liver function.

2. Prevention (Lifestyle & Hygiene)

Preventing dermatophytosis is all about "Starving the Fungus" of the moisture and contact it needs to spread.

Personal Hygiene

  • Keep it Dry: Fungus loves dampness. Dry your skin thoroughly after showering, especially between the toes and in skin folds (groin, armpits).
  • Loose Clothing: Wear breathable cotton fabrics and avoid tight synthetic underwear or socks that trap sweat.
  • Daily Laundry: Wash your towels, bedding, and clothes in hot water during an active infection to kill fungal spores.

Environmental Control

  • Don't Share: Never share personal items like towels, razors, hairbrushes, or unwashed gym gear.
  • Footwear in Public: Always wear flip-flops or sandals in communal showers, locker rooms, and around swimming pools.
  • Disinfect Surfaces: Clean shared gym equipment or mats before use.

Animal Safety

  • Check Pets: If your cat or dog has patches of missing fur or is scratching excessively, take them to the vet. Pets are a primary source of transmission to humans.
